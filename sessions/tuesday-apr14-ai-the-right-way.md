## Notes

### AI Is Fundamentally Different from Prior Tech Waves
- AI is not like cloud computing or Google search — the comparison is misleading
  - Google search that fails just returns bad results; no decision-making, no assumptions
  - AI is agentic — it reasons, makes decisions, builds on machine learning models
  - Bad AI output can come across as **very convincing and fluent** — unlike a failed search, you may not realize it's wrong
  - Error profile is "incomparable" to prior technology — AI errors are symmetric (look correct) vs. search errors (obviously wrong)
- **World Economic Forum** data: AI will likely generate more jobs than it displaces, similar to other technology waves — it's reskilling, not replacement (referenced **Displacement Service Theory**)
- Reskilling existing jobs — leveraging AI tools to shift job responsibilities rather than replacing roles
- Thinking about ethical concerns — antitrust, privacy, bias, autonomy, misinformation at scale
- Existential risks of AI
- We are at an inflection point — scattershot AI adoption is now becoming more focused and intentional

### Literary & Cultural Influences on AI Perception
- Referenced Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics as a framework for AI safety (from *I, Robot*, 1950):
  1. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm
  2. A robot must obey orders given by human beings, except where such orders would conflict with the First Law
  3. A robot must protect its own existence, as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law
- Authors/works have been exploring AI and computers taking over since the 1890s — *Erewhon*, *I, Robot*, Arthur C. Clarke, *2001*, etc.
- We are all influenced by what we read and see — "Terminator, Skynet" framing affects how the public (and patrons) think about AI
- Libraries as **trusted civic hubs** are the right place for patrons to learn about AI with ethics and transparency

### 2025 Pulse of the Library
- Referenced the **2025 Pulse of the Library** report (Clarivate annual survey of library professionals)
  - 400+ public libraries responded
  - **67%** of libraries globally are exploring or implementing AI tools
  - **56%** recognize AI will require significant upskilling or reskilling
  - Growing interest in AI but cautious, uneven adoption
  - Use cases: cataloging, metadata, patron services, collection analytics
  - Concerns: data privacy, algorithmic bias, accuracy/hallucinations, ethics in public-serving institutions
  - Gap between institutional interest in AI and staff readiness — calls for more training and governance frameworks
  - **Biggest concerns:** security and privacy — where does the information go?
  - Environmental impacts of AI (energy/compute costs) — but possibly offset by efficiencies AI unlocks
  - Environmental concern came up prominently at PLA — 3 different customers raised it, largely from areas where data centers are being built
  - Counterpoint: data centers were already being built during Big Data era; AI may actually help reduce environmental impact (e.g., supply chain emissions reduction through prescriptive analytics)
  - **Budget concerns:** Clarivate's current AI features (Data Explorer, Metadata Assistant, etc.) are included under existing licenses — no extra cost

### Keys to Successful AI Adoption
- Libraries that have seen success have **strategic leadership** behind AI adoption — someone championing and making decisions about AI
- **Invest in training and development** — Clarivate has invested in learning internally; seeing same at successful libraries
- Need to **close the implementation gap** — don't just dive in with scattershot AI adoption
- Some orgs have really jumped in, but that's not easy unless you deeply understand the problems
  - Proof of concept approach — and it's OK if a POC isn't successful; that's the point
  - **"Fail fast"** — audience comment (Ray): achieve a failure state quickly; it's much quicker to develop with AI; what didn't work is sometimes more valuable than anything else
  - AI is also useful for **problem refinement** — "here is the problem I'm having, what are the data points or KPIs I need?"
  - Can use AI to generate project plans, checklists, and identify patterns
- Having an AI policy in place can help provide guidance for adoption — 2–3 recent contract renewals already include AI transparency policies

### Related: Simon Willison's Principles for Responsible AI

Simon Willison (developer, blogger at simonwillison.net) has been a prominent voice on practical responsible AI use:

- **Transparency** — always disclose when AI was used; label AI-assisted work
- **Human accountability** — you remain responsible for AI output; AI is a tool, you are the author
- **Don't trust, verify** — LLMs hallucinate; treat output as a first draft requiring fact-checking
- **Augment, don't replace, judgment** — AI works best for tasks you already understand; using it where you can't evaluate correctness is dangerous
- **Attribution & IP** — respect provenance of training data; be cautious about reproducing copyrighted material
- **Experimentation over hype** — ground claims in what LLMs actually do, not speculative capabilities

Core theme: **the human stays in the loop and stays accountable.**
